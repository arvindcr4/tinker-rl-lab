## Discovery 1: Standard GRPO Ablation Hyperparameters in DeepSeek, Qwen, and Related LLMs

### Summary

Across GRPO-style training reports for DeepSeek, Qwen, and adjacent LLMs, three hyperparameters show convergent practice: group size, learning rate, and sampling temperature. Reported group sizes cluster at 8–64 rollouts per prompt, learning rates at 1×10<sup>-6</sup>–5×10<sup>-6</sup>, and training-time temperatures at 0.7–1.2, whereas the KL coefficient is inconsistent and often set to zero. These conclusions reflect a synthesis of open technical reports rather than a single curated benchmark.

### Background

Grouped Reinforcement Policy Optimization (GRPO) replaces a learned value baseline with within-group reward normalization, enabling on-policy reinforcement learning for LLMs without a separate critic. As GRPO has been adopted for mathematical and general reasoning across DeepSeek, Qwen, and related ecosystems, reproducible training hinges on a small set of rollout and optimization hyperparameters that govern stability, exploration, and compute usage. Establishing standard practice for these hyperparameters—and highlighting where it does not yet exist—provides immediate guidance for ablation design and clarifies gaps that limit fair comparison across algorithms and labs.

### Results & Discussion

The compiled evidence indicates narrow, recurring ranges for three GRPO ablation hyperparameters. First, reported rollouts per prompt (group size) concentrate at 8, 16, or 64: examples include 16 for DeepSeek-R1-Zero, 64 for DeepSeekMath-RL 7B, 16 for JT-Math, and 8 for Qwen/Llama small-scale R1-style runs and a Qwen-adjacent GRPO baseline [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [guo2025deepseekr1incentivizesreasoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09422-z.pdf), [shao2024deepseekmathpushingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2402.03300), hao2025jtmathamultistage, [chen2505towardsrevealingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.17988?), fang2511domathreasoning]. Second, learning rates consistently lie in the low-micro regime, typically 1×10<sup>-6</sup>–5×10<sup>-6</sup>, with DeepSeek-R1-Zero at 3×10<sup>-6</sup>, DeepSeekMath at 1×10<sup>-6</sup>, JT-Math at 4×10<sup>-6</sup>, the R1-style study at 2.5×10<sup>-6</sup>–5×10<sup>-6</sup>, and a GR/regularization study selecting 5×10<sup>-6</sup> or 3×10<sup>-6</sup> depending on the regularizer [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [guo2025deepseekr1incentivizesreasoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09422-z.pdf), [shao2024deepseekmathpushingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2402.03300), hao2025jtmathamultistage, [chen2505towardsrevealingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.17988?), ackermann2026gradientregularizationprevents]. Third, training-time sampling temperatures cluster between 0.7 and 1.2, with explicit settings at 0.7 (R1-style Qwen/Llama), 1.0 (DeepSeek-R1-Zero and a Qwen-adjacent GRPO baseline), and 1.2 (JT-Math) [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [chen2505towardsrevealingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.17988?), [guo2025deepseekr1incentivizesreasoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09422-z.pdf), hao2025jtmathamultistage, fang2511domathreasoning]. In contrast, the KL divergence coefficient shows no consensus: some works set β=0 or omit the term, while others tune it over orders of magnitude, from 1×10<sup>-3</sup> to 4×10<sup>-2</sup> or even 0.03–0.15 in GRPO-style RLHF studies [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [he2025skyworkopenreasoner](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.22312), [chen2505towardsrevealingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.17988?), nguyen2026adaptiverolloutallocation, fang2511domathreasoning, [guo2025deepseekr1incentivizesreasoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09422-z.pdf), [shao2024deepseekmathpushingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2402.03300), hao2025jtmathamultistage, ackermann2026gradientregularizationprevents]. Together, these reports define practical “defaults” for group size, learning rate, and temperature, and underscore the absence of a standard KL setting in GRPO ablations.

The examples further clarify how these settings are used and justified. DeepSeek-R1-Zero adopts GRPO with grouped rewards for advantage computation (G=16, T=1.0, β=0.001, LR=3×10<sup>-6</sup>) and reports strong reasoning gains under these on-policy rollouts [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [guo2025deepseekr1incentivizesreasoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09422-z.pdf)]. DeepSeekMath reports a larger group size (G=64) and a higher KL (β=0.04) with LR=1×10<sup>-6</sup>, achieving 88.2% on GSM8K and 51.7% on MATH, and emphasizes that the group-average reward baseline obviates a learned critic while normalizing rewards within the group [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [shao2024deepseekmathpushingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2402.03300)]. JT-Math uses strictly on-policy GRPO with G=16, T=1.2, β=1×10<sup>-3</sup>, and LR=4×10<sup>-6</sup>, after a targeted search over rollout count, learning rate, and clipping to avoid entropy collapse; the authors explicitly reduced KL to encourage exploration within a progressive length curriculum [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), hao2025jtmathamultistage]. In a small-scale R1-style study on Qwen/Llama, the KL term is removed (G=8, T=0.7, LR=2.5×10<sup>-6</sup>–5×10<sup>-6</sup>), and higher rollout temperature (1.2) increases entropy without meaningfully improving accuracy, highlighting the exploration–stability trade-off in on-policy sampling [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [chen2505towardsrevealingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.17988?)]. Complementing these, a Qwen-adjacent GRPO baseline retains a small KL (β=1×10<sup>-3</sup>) with G=8, T=1.0, and LR=1×10<sup>-6</sup>, and explicitly uses a low-variance KL surrogate [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), fang2511domathreasoning].

The role of the KL coefficient is particularly heterogeneous. Several GRPO-style pipelines either explicitly remove KL (β=0) or find it counterproductive in multi-stage training: Skywork-OR1 reports that a KL penalty hinders improvement and so omits it, while a theoretical analysis of adaptive rollout allocation also sets β=0 for simplicity [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [he2025skyworkopenreasoner](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.22312), nguyen2026adaptiverolloutallocation]. Where KL is retained, it is tuned as a free knob with widely varying magnitudes, from 1×10<sup>-3</sup> in multiple reasoning setups to 4×10<sup>-2</sup> in DeepSeekMath, and up to 0.03–0.15 in a GRPO-style RLHF regimen on Qwen/Pythia backbones [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [guo2025deepseekr1incentivizesreasoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09422-z.pdf), hao2025jtmathamultistage, fang2511domathreasoning, [shao2024deepseekmathpushingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2402.03300), ackermann2026gradientregularizationprevents]. Training temperature, in turn, is consistently reported in the 0.7–1.2 band, with some evidence that higher temperatures may depress early accuracy yet improve final gains if entropy is otherwise controlled or if training remains strictly on-policy [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [he2025skyworkopenreasoner](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.22312), [chen2505towardsrevealingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.17988?), hao2025jtmathamultistage]. Taken together, these patterns suggest that community defaults for group size, learning rate, and temperature emerge from stability and compute constraints of on-policy RL, while the KL term functions as an optional regularizer that teams either disable to avoid prematurely constraining exploration or actively tune across a broad range [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2)].

This synthesis used a descriptive “range hypothesis check” rather than inferential statistics because few reports disclose all four hyperparameters, leaving inference underpowered; consequently, the claims here are range-based and anchored in explicit numeric disclosures from each paper [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2)]. The compilation also exposes recurring evaluation and reporting gaps likely to attract reviewer scrutiny in major venues: incomplete hyperparameter sheets (e.g., temperature or rollout counts not reported), attribution ambiguities (e.g., Qwen backbones trained outside official reports), and reliance on arXiv technical reports rather than peer-reviewed proceedings; for example, CPPO-related work discusses group size and KL but omits full numerical settings in the accessible excerpts, and Skywork-OR1 provides qualitative rationales without a complete numeric grid [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2), [lin2503cppoacceleratingthe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.22342), [he2025skyworkopenreasoner](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.22312)]. In short, the table establishes practical ablation defaults for GRPO—group size 8–64, learning rate 1×10<sup>-6</sup>–5×10<sup>-6</sup>, temperature 0.7–1.2—while highlighting that KL usage is non-standard and that reproducibility is limited by underreported configurations and non-uniform evaluation protocols across the current open literature [[r1](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/8ababd1c-5279-4042-bf3c-a4c5dcf227d2)].

## Discovery 2: Principal Reviewer Objections to RL-for-LLM Papers at Major Conferences

### Summary

Across OpenReview peer reviews at major ML conferences, insufficient evaluation emerges as the dominant reviewer objection for RL-for-LLM submissions, appearing in 86.3% of analyzed reviews and substantially outpacing concerns about novelty, reproducibility, theory, generalization, compute cost, instability, or reward hacking. The analysis is based on large, structured review datasets and a combined topic-modeling plus manual-coding pipeline, indicating that evaluation adequacy is the primary barrier to acceptance for this research area.

### Background

Reinforcement learning for large language models has accelerated alongside alignment and instruction-following methods, yet clear standards for empirical validation have lagged behind rapid algorithmic innovation. Peer review corpora offer a unique lens into what experts perceive as necessary evidence for rigor and impact, spanning adequacy of baselines, generalization under distribution shift, computational efficiency, and stability of training procedures. Systematically mining these reviews can thus illuminate recurring evaluation gaps and help align future submissions with evolving expectations in the field.

### Results & Discussion

Two complementary OpenReview datasets on HuggingFace were identified as suitable for large-scale analysis of reviewer criticisms in RL-for-LLM work: AlgorithmicResearchGroup/openreview-papers-with-reviews (98,124 reviews from 28,329 papers across 171 venues, 2017–2023) and smallari/openreview-iclr2024-peer-reviews-RAW (7,404 ICLR 2024 papers), together covering ~2,260 RL+LLM papers and ~12,029 reviews with full review text and rich metadata [[r3](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/70a16f15-1285-487f-bc27-4c19e3ea3ab7)]. The subsequent analysis used the first dataset to extract RL-for-LLM submissions via title/abstract keyword filters, yielding 2,581 reviews, of which 490 contained explicit or mined “weakness” content spanning 248 unique papers (2017–2023; 79.6% from 2020–2023). Themes were derived via a two-stage process: Latent Dirichlet Allocation (10 topics) to surface coherent clusters, followed by qualitative coding into eight predefined categories with multiple labels allowed per review (mean 3.61 themes/review; 97.1% of reviews exhibited at least one theme) [[r6](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/98c9b4e3-c366-4a8b-816d-f442078bd530)]. While a recent ICLR 2024 dataset was also identified, it was not used in this analysis due to loading issues in that pipeline [[r6](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/98c9b4e3-c366-4a8b-816d-f442078bd530)].

The most frequent criticism by a wide margin was insufficient evaluation, coded in 86.3% of reviews, often phrased as calls to strengthen the empirical premise and evidential support (for example, “the experiments supporting the signficance of the method need further strengthening”) [[r6](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/98c9b4e3-c366-4a8b-816d-f442078bd530)]. Regarding training stability and comparability, reviewers recommended side-by-side, “online” evaluations across objectives to clarify behavioral differences [[r6](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/98c9b4e3-c366-4a8b-816d-f442078bd530)]. The analysis benefits from multi-venue coverage and structured review texts, but several design choices bound its scope. RL-for-LLM papers were identified via title/abstract keyword filters rather than full-text search, and “weakness” content was compiled from both explicit sections and mined critical sentences, yielding 490 analyzable reviews from 248 papers out of 2,581 total reviews in the filtered set [[r6](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/98c9b4e3-c366-4a8b-816d-f442078bd530)]. Topic modeling and coding parameters (e.g., LDA topics, vectorization thresholds) and the allowance of multiple theme labels per review shape observed frequencies; no formal significance testing was performed [[r6](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/98c9b4e3-c366-4a8b-816d-f442078bd530)]. Two OpenReview datasets were available (2017–2024), but only one was used for the theme analysis; nonetheless, both datasets meet completeness criteria (full review texts, venue/year metadata, titles/abstracts) and enable cross-venue comparisons and temporal extensions in follow-up work [[r3](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/70a16f15-1285-487f-bc27-4c19e3ea3ab7), [r6](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/98c9b4e3-c366-4a8b-816d-f442078bd530)]. While the original objective includes NeurIPS/ICML 2025–2026, the present sources cover 2017–2024; extending this analysis to newer cycles would be straightforward once those review corpora are available [[r3](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/70a16f15-1285-487f-bc27-4c19e3ea3ab7)].

Practical implications for authors are immediate. The dominance of insufficient evaluation signals that submissions should prioritize stronger empirical premises, stress-testing generalization across datasets and shifts, reporting compute and memory costs, and clarifying online versus offline comparisons to illuminate objective behavior; in RL settings, explicitly separating evaluation metrics from reward signals reduces ambiguity about true task competence [[r6](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/98c9b4e3-c366-4a8b-816d-f442078bd530)]. By contrast, the first query—standard ablation configurations for GRPO (e.g., group size, temperature, KL coefficient, learning rate) in DeepSeek-R1, Qwen, and related work—cannot be answered from peer-review text alone; assembling that table requires extracting hyperparameters from the primary papers rather than OpenReview metadata and reviews, which were used here solely to analyze criticism themes [[r3](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/70a16f15-1285-487f-bc27-4c19e3ea3ab7), [r6](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/98c9b4e3-c366-4a8b-816d-f442078bd530)].

## Discovery 3: Divergence Between Author Evaluation Practices and Reviewer Priorities in RL-for-LLM

### Summary

Across RL-for-LLM, authors commonly emphasize human and LLM-judge evaluations, community benchmarks, and KL/β ablations, yet reviewers predominantly reward strong baseline coverage and comprehensive ablation suites. A large-scale rating analysis finds no statistically significant uplift associated with including human evaluation, capability or preference benchmarks, or KL/β ablations, indicating a systematic mismatch between author practices and reviewer priorities.

### Background

Reinforcement learning for large language models (LLMs) has coalesced around preference-driven training and evaluation, pairing canonical preference datasets (for example, TL;DR summarization and HH/HH-RLHF dialogue) with capability and safety suites (for example, MMLU, TruthfulQA, and HumanEval). Human preference studies and, increasingly, LLM-as-judge protocols are used to scale evaluation, while ablations around conservativeness (KL/β), data mixtures, and decoding are treated as core stability checks. Yet conference acceptance decisions ultimately reflect reviewer expectations, motivating a quantitative reconciliation of what authors report versus what reviewers request.

### Results & Discussion

A systematic extraction across 24 high-impact RL-for-LLM papers identifies a partial but clear consensus on “what good looks like” in author evaluations: preference datasets such as TL;DR and HH/HH-RLHF recur across method families including RLAIF and DPO, while capability/safety suites such as TruthfulQA, HumanEval, and MMLU are frequently reported in alignment- and instruction-tuning works including InstructGPT, Anthropic’s Helpful/Harmless assistant, Tulu 2, and RLHFlow [[r5](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/abd8a686-28bd-49d5-958f-03a351d425ed), [lee2023rlaifscalingreinforcement](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2309.00267), [rafailov2024directpreferenceoptimization](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper\_files/paper/2023/file/a85b405ed65c6477a4fe8302b5e06ce7-Paper-Conference.pdf), [dong2023raftrewardranked](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2304.06767), [ouyang2022traininglanguagemodels](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.02155), [bai2022trainingahelpful](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2204.05862), [ivison2023camelsina](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2311.10702), [dong2024rlhfworkflowfrom](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2405.07863)]. Human evaluation is predominantly pairwise (A/B) with Elo or win-rate aggregation—exemplified by Anthropic’s assistants, WebGPT, Constitutional AI, DPO’s human validation, and ChatGLM-RLHF—and LLM-as-judge evaluation is now standard in many post-2023 works, supported by >80% agreement with human preferences on MT-Bench/Arena; in the extracted corpus, explicit human evaluation is present in 46% of papers (Wilson 95% CI 0.28–0.65) and LLM-as-judge in 33% (Wilson 95% CI 0.18–0.52) [[r5](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/abd8a686-28bd-49d5-958f-03a351d425ed), [bai2022trainingahelpful](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2204.05862), [nakano2021webgptbrowserassistedquestionanswering](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2112.09332), [bai2212constitutionalaiharmlessness](https://ai-plans.com/file\_storage/4f32fa39-3a01-46c7-878e-c92b7aa7165f\_2212.08073v1.pdf), [rafailov2024directpreferenceoptimization](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper\_files/paper/2023/file/a85b405ed65c6477a4fe8302b5e06ce7-Paper-Conference.pdf), [hou2024chatglmrlhfpracticesof](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2404.00934), [zheng2023judgingllmasajudgewith](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2306.05685)]. Across ablations, “conservativeness control” via KL or β is among the most recurrent knobs (appearing in 33% of the corpus; Wilson 95% CI 0.18–0.52), spanning RLAIF’s β discussion, DPO β sweeps, PPO implementations (trlX; Secrets of RLHF), WebGPT’s KL studies, and simple PPO variants (ReMax), alongside systematic decoding/best-of-N sweeps and component/data-scaling ablations [[r5](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/abd8a686-28bd-49d5-958f-03a351d425ed), [lee2023rlaifscalingreinforcement](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2309.00267), [rafailov2024directpreferenceoptimization](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper\_files/paper/2023/file/a85b405ed65c6477a4fe8302b5e06ce7-Paper-Conference.pdf), [havrilla2023trlxaframework](https://aclanthology.org/2023.emnlp-main.530.pdf), [zheng2023secretsofrlhf](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2307.04964), [nakano2021webgptbrowserassistedquestionanswering](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2112.09332), [li2023remaxasimple](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2310.10505), [bai2022trainingahelpful](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2204.05862)].

Despite these de facto author standards, a large OpenReview-based rating analysis of 356 RL-for-LLM papers (2020–2024) shows no statistically significant association between including human evaluation, capability benchmarks, preference benchmarks, or KL/β ablations and higher reviewer ratings (Mann–Whitney U, all p > 0.05 with FDR correction) [[r7](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7a5fdec6-ac13-4d95-a712-1e4fcd8154f7)]. Mean ratings were indistinguishable for human evaluation (with 5.681 ± 0.825 vs without 5.732 ± 1.160; Δ = −0.051; Cohen’s d = −0.046; p = 0.265), capability benchmarks (with 5.927 ± 0.976 vs without 5.718 ± 1.105; Δ = +0.209; d = 0.190; p = 0.338), and preference benchmarks (with 5.777 ± 0.713 vs without 5.717 ± 1.134; Δ = +0.060; d = 0.054; p = 0.986); KL/β ablations exhibited a larger effect size (with 6.667 ± 0.722, n = 3, vs without 5.714 ± 1.101; Δ = +0.952; d = 0.866) but remained underpowered (p = 0.087) [[r7](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7a5fdec6-ac13-4d95-a712-1e4fcd8154f7)]. Complementary t-tests and year-controlled regressions yield the same conclusion (all p > 0.05; full model R² = 0.011), indicating these widely implemented evaluation components explain virtually none of the variance in reviewer ratings in this dataset [[r7](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7a5fdec6-ac13-4d95-a712-1e4fcd8154f7)].

Direct analysis of reviewer text further clarifies priorities: among 356 criticism sentences drawn from 466 reviews of 398 papers (ICLR and NeurIPS, 2017–2025), the top evaluation gap by a wide margin is missing baseline comparisons (220 mentions; 56.8%), followed by missing ablations (56; 14.5%), limited/weak evaluation (49; 12.7%), and missing/limited benchmarks in general (41; 10.6%) [[r8](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7100b5e2-7670-4d99-a097-d55d989aba74)]. Notably, missing human evaluation is rarely flagged (8 mentions; 2.1%), and reviewers seldom demand specific benchmarks outright (HumanEval 1.6%, MT-Bench 0.8%, MMLU 0.5%); the pattern is stable across 2024 (58.9% baseline complaints) and 2025 (56.4%), and most baseline critiques are generic (72.7%) rather than specifying a comparison type (27.3%) [[r8](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7100b5e2-7670-4d99-a097-d55d989aba74)]. Together with the rating analysis, these results indicate that resource-intensive human or LLM-judge studies and the inclusion of capability/preference benchmarks, while common among authors, are not the principal determinants of reviewer scoring; instead, breadth and rigor of baseline and ablation coverage dominate reviewer concerns [[r7](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7a5fdec6-ac13-4d95-a712-1e4fcd8154f7), [r8](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7100b5e2-7670-4d99-a097-d55d989aba74)].

Two practical implications follow. First, to align with reviewer priorities at NeurIPS/ICML, the highest-return evaluation investments appear to be (i) strong, diverse baselines (including prior SOTA methods, prompting systems, and alternative RL/post-training approaches) and (ii) a comprehensive, sensitivity-oriented ablation suite—especially around KL/β, decoding, data mixtures, and algorithmic components—while human evaluation can be treated as supportive rather than essential given how rarely its absence is criticized [[r5](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/abd8a686-28bd-49d5-958f-03a351d425ed), [r7](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7a5fdec6-ac13-4d95-a712-1e4fcd8154f7), [r8](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7100b5e2-7670-4d99-a097-d55d989aba74)]. Second, with respect to Query 1 (GRPO-specific ablation configurations: group size, temperature, KL coefficient, learning rate), the present corpus did not include GRPO-focused reports such as DeepSeek-R1 or Qwen, so a table of standard GRPO settings could not be compiled from these sources; nonetheless, the cross-paper ablation patterns above (KL/β sweeps, best-of-N/decoding scans, and component/data-scaling studies) provide a ready-made blueprint for GRPO evaluation once those papers are incorporated [[r5](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/abd8a686-28bd-49d5-958f-03a351d425ed)]. The systematic extraction’s documented limitations (incomplete coverage of some experiments sections) and the low prevalence of some practices in the rating analysis caution that additional targeted curation—explicitly including GRPO case studies—will be required to refine hyperparameter norms without altering the core reviewer–author divergence reported here [[r5](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/abd8a686-28bd-49d5-958f-03a351d425ed), [r7](https://platform.edisonscientific.com/kosmos/862f321d-2fe8-48be-ae0b-0d36c3fb049e/trajectories/7a5fdec6-ac13-4d95-a712-1e4fcd8154f7)].
