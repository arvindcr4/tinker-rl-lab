\begin{abstract}
This paper is an engineering diagnostic for group-relative reinforcement
learning for LLMs (RLVR), not a benchmark or leaderboard.  We study whether a
critic-free group-relative update has a usable learning signal under a given
model--reward--sampler--stack combination.

\textbf{Contribution: a diagnostic and reporting framework.}  First, we
introduce a mixed-group probability mechanism:
\[
P(\text{usable}) \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_x \left[1 - (1-p_x)^G - p_x^G\right],
\]
where $p_x$ is the current policy's success rate on prompt $x$ and $G$ is the
group size.  This shows that a group-relative update has usable signal only
when the policy samples mixed-reward groups; all-identical rewards yield zero
gradient.  The familiar $p_x > 1/G$ rule is only an expected-one-success
heuristic, not a threshold theorem.

Second, we operationalize this as Zero-Variance Fraction (ZVF) and Gradient
Utilization (GU) as \emph{triage diagnostics}, not capability metrics.  ZVF
measures the fraction of prompts where all $G$ completions receive identical
rewards; GU $= 1 - $ZVF measures usable signal fraction.  High ZVF with low
reward indicates cold-start collapse; high ZVF with high reward indicates
saturation; low ZVF indicates within-group contrast.  A fixed first-five-step
rule (ZVF $\geq 80\%$ with reward $\leq 5\%$) catches all collapsed runs in a
22-run validation set (precision 1.0, recall 1.0), but only two positive
failures are observed and continuous ZVF ranking is weak.  The defensible
claim is triage, not prediction.  Importantly, ZVF is task-driven: tool-use
experiments saturate at ZVF $= 100\%$ regardless of model scale, while GSM8K
experiments average ZVF $= 8.5\%$.

Third, we show that training reward conflates with held-out capability.  The
only clean held-out capability result is a paired GSM8K 200-example evaluation:
Qwen3-8B improves only $82.0\% \to 83.3\%$ ($p = 0.26$), a small and
non-significant gain.  Four Llama-3.3-70B seeds show training last-10 values
ranging 95.0--98.1\% yet all land on 95.0\% held-out accuracy, demonstrating
that last-10 variance reflects Monte-Carlo noise rather than genuine
generalization differences.  Online training reward is dynamics evidence,
not capability evidence.

Fourth, we show that algorithm labels (PPO, GRPO, DPO) are under-specified
experimental treatments.  Under nominally matched visible configurations
(Qwen3-8B, $G=8$, lr$=10^{-5}$, GSM8K, 30 steps, seed 42), the Tinker runner
reaches 84.4\% last-10 training reward while TRL on Modal H100 reaches 5.0\%.
This 17$\times$ gap does not rank frameworks; it proves that backend, sampler,
reward parser, LoRA configuration, optimizer, and evaluator are co-determinants
of outcome in ways that hyperparameter tables do not capture.

\textbf{What we do not claim:} We do not claim this runner represents canonical
GRPO.  We do not claim broad reasoning improvement or state-of-the-art GSM8K/MATH
performance.  We do not claim ZVF predicts final performance.  We do not claim
a universal PPO-versus-GRPO ranking.  We do not claim tool-execution competence;
tool-call results measure schema compliance only.  We do not claim frontier
scaling laws from short, noisy training traces.

Our contribution is a diagnostic protocol: how to decide whether a specific
model--reward--sampler--stack combination has usable group-relative RL signal,
and how to scope claims accordingly.  We release the code, run logs, and
checkpoints at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/tinker-rl-lab}.
\end{abstract}
