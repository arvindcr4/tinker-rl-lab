\begin{abstract}
GRPO is often discussed as a reasoning-improvement algorithm, but sparse
verifiable rewards only create a useful update when sampled groups contain
reward contrast.  We present \ours{}, an exploratory systems study of 79
post-training runs across tool-call formatting, GSM8K, MATH-style prompts,
HumanEval-style verification, multiple model families, and several training
stacks.  The main contribution is not a leaderboard.  It is a diagnostic
account of when standard GRPO has a learning signal.

First, Zero-Variance Fraction (ZVF) and Gradient Utilization (GU) are best
treated as early triage diagnostics rather than calibrated performance
predictors.  High ZVF with low reward flags cold-start collapse; high ZVF
with high reward flags saturation; low ZVF indicates usable within-group
contrast.  A fixed first-five-step rule catches both collapsed runs in a
22-run de-duplicated validation set with no false positives, but only two
positive failures are observed, and continuous ZVF ranking is weak.
Second, the relevant mechanism is mixed-reward sampling probability:
\[
P(\text{usable}) \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_x \left[1 - (1-p_x)^G - p_x^G\right].
\]
The familiar $1/G$ rule is only an expected-one-success heuristic.  This
explains why SFT warm-up, group size, reward density, and task difficulty
matter more than task labels.  Third, PPO/GRPO/DPO labels are incomplete
experimental treatments unless the model family, backend, reward parser,
sampler, optimizer, checkpoint rule, and evaluation harness are specified.
Our Qwen PPO/GRPO comparison is artifact-sensitive, and our Llama comparison
favors PPO; neither supports a universal algorithm ranking.

Held-out GSM8K improves only from 82.0\% to 83.3\% ($p=0.26$), so we do not
claim significant held-out math improvement.  The defensible conclusion is
that GRPO is a reward-contrast amplifier: useful when the policy already
samples distinguishable outcomes, brittle when the reward distribution is
flat.
\end{abstract}
