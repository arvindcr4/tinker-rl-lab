\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We asked a simple question---\emph{what actually drives performance in RL
post-training?}---and answered it with 79 controlled runs.  Five findings
stand.
\textbf{F1 (ZVF early-warning diagnostic):} ZVF correlates with final
reward at $r{=}{-}0.769$ ($p{=}0.0008$), but lagged regression reveals
this is largely explained by current reward level (partial $r{=}0.059$ at
lag 5). ZVF retains marginal independent signal at longer horizons (lag 10:
partial $r{=}0.080$, $p{=}0.045$), and all runs with sustained ZVF
$\geq 80\%$ in the first 5 steps collapsed---making it a practical triage
tool rather than an independent causal predictor.
\textbf{F2 (inverted-U in group size):} $G{=}32$ is the sweet spot; both
smaller and larger $G$ waste compute, refuting the ``more rollouts is better''
folklore.
\textbf{F3 (base vs.\ instruct dichotomy):} the $+0.922$ SFT delta dwarfs
every RL contribution we measure and gates trainability below 8B-instruct
across Qwen and Llama.
\textbf{F4 (PPO vs.\ GRPO may be model-dependent):} the ranking reverses
between Qwen3-8B and Llama-3.1-8B, but with only two single-seed model
families (MDE $d{=}2.024$), this remains a hypothesis requiring
replication, not a conclusion.
\textbf{F5 (Ceiling stability varies across architectures):} scale alone does
not buy stability at the 30-step horizon, but Kimi-K2-class pre-training
recipes do---Nemotron-120B collapses where Kimi-K2 and Qwen3-235B-A22B hold
their ceilings.  (All single-seed, early-training observations.)
\textbf{F6 (Cold-Start Capability Threshold, validated):} the probability
of a mixed-reward group $P(\text{usable})\!=\!1-(1-p_0)^{G}-p_0^{G}$
quantitatively predicts the task hierarchy we observe---tool-use
(no warm-up) and binary-reward HumanEval sit at $P(\text{usable})\!\approx\!0$
and fail; SFT-warmed tool-use, GSM8K on Qwen3-8B, and dense-reward
HumanEval all sit at $P(\text{usable})\!\approx\!1$ and learn.  The familiar
$1/G$ bound is the small-$p_0$ limit; the closed-form predictor is the
mechanism.
\textbf{F7 (Framework is a first-class variable):} the PPO/GRPO ranking
flips between Qwen3-8B and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, and TRL-GRPO diverges
from Tinker-GRPO under matched compute---library ablations belong
alongside every algorithmic comparison.

\paragraph{The open question.}
Why does the model--algorithm interaction (F4) exist at all?  We conjecture
that initial reward-landscape geometry---proxied by zero-shot task
accuracy---determines whether GRPO's variance-reduced group contrast or PPO's
value-function smoothing is preferable.  This is directly testable with the
LoRA adapters and step-level traces we release, and would convert F4 from an
empirical curiosity into a predictive rule.

\paragraph{What the artifacts enable.}
Every run, log, and checkpoint in this paper is public.  Researchers can
(i)~calibrate AERO~\citep{zhang2026aero}'s Beta prior against our cross-scale
ZVF traces; (ii)~test whether the 2-GRPO/DPO
equivalence~\citep{wu2025grpo_dpo} extrapolates across F4's direction flip;
(iii)~probe whether F5's sustained-ceiling behaviour is a property of agentic
RL pre-training or of MoE routing; (iv)~falsify the reward-landscape
conjecture with gradient-level analysis~\citep{liu2025rlsubnetworks};
(v)~run the pre-registered $G\!=\!256$ large-group and dense partial-reward
HumanEval rescues that directly test F6's $1/G$ boundary; and
(vi)~re-run the F7 PPO/GRPO flip across TRL, veRL, and OpenRLHF to
separate algorithm from framework.  RL
post-training is no longer a niche technique---it is how the most capable
language models learn to reason.  F1--F7 are the first controlled
cross-library, cross-family map of that regime; the hardest and most
important work is the mechanistic follow-up they make possible.
