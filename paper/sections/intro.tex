\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

PPO~\citep{schulman2017proximal}, GRPO~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath}, and
DPO~\citep{rafailov2024direct} are often compared as if the algorithm label
were the full experimental treatment.  In LLM post-training, outcomes also
depend on base model, instruction tuning, reward parser, group construction,
sampler, backend, LoRA configuration, checkpoint selection, and evaluation
harness.  This is the LLM post-training analogue of the reproducibility
concerns Henderson et al. identified for deep RL~\citep{henderson2018deep}:
algorithm labels are under-specified unless the full training stack is fixed
or modeled.

This paper audits a group-relative RL runner inspired by GRPO, not a
canonical GRPO reproduction.  It retains group-normalized advantages and a
critic-free structure, but does not implement the PPO ratio and clip of
\citet{schulman2017proximal}, does not retain a frozen reference policy for
KL regularization, performs one optimizer step per rollout batch, and does
not use a completion-only token mask.  A P1-A diagnostic on Qwen3.5-4B
measures 61.6--89.6\% of the full-sequence loss magnitude from prompt tokens
rather than completion tokens.  Claims in this paper concern this runner,
not canonical GRPO.

The paper does not show that this runner broadly improves reasoning.  The
only clean held-out capability result is a paired GSM8K 200-example evaluation
that improves only $82.0\% \to 83.3\%$ ($p = 0.26$); we do not claim a
significant held-out math effect.  The paper's positive contribution is a
diagnostic protocol: how to decide whether a specific model--reward--sampler--
stack combination has usable group-relative RL signal, and how to scope
claims accordingly.

\subsection{Claim 1: Reward-contrast amplifier mechanism.}
A group-relative update has useful signal only when the sampled group contains
distinguishable reward outcomes.  Under binary or verifier-like rewards, if
the current policy succeeds on prompt $x$ with probability $p_x$ and group size
is $G$, the probability of a mixed, gradient-carrying group is
\[
P(\text{usable}) \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_x \left[1 - (1-p_x)^G - p_x^G\right].
\]
The familiar $p_x > 1/G$ rule is only an expected-one-success heuristic; it
is not a threshold theorem.  This mechanism explains why warm-starts, group
size, reward density, and task difficulty matter more than task labels.
When ZVF $= 1$ (all groups identical-reward), no gradient flows; when ZVF $= 0$
(all groups mixed-reward), the update exploits within-group contrast.

\subsection{Claim 2: ZVF/GU as triage diagnostics.}
Zero-Variance Fraction (ZVF) measures the fraction of prompts where all $G$
completions receive identical rewards, producing zero gradient.
Gradient Utilization (GU) $= 1 - $ZVF measures the fraction of prompts
providing usable signal.  High ZVF with low reward indicates cold-start
collapse; high ZVF with high reward indicates saturation; low ZVF indicates
within-group contrast.  A fixed first-five-step rule (ZVF $\geq 80\%$, reward
$\leq 5\%$) catches both collapsed runs in a 22-run de-duplicated validation
set with no false positives, but only two positive failures are observed and
continuous ZVF ranking is weak.  The defensible claim is triage, not
prediction: ZVF/GU can support early decisions to stop, warm-start, densify
reward, adjust task difficulty, or change group size.  Importantly, ZVF is
task-driven, not model-scale-driven: tool-use experiments saturate at ZVF $= 100\%$
regardless of model size, while GSM8K experiments average ZVF $= 8.5\%$.

The mixed-group probability formula is the explanatory core; ZVF/GU is the
operational interface.

\subsection{Claim 3: Training reward is not capability.}
Online reward curves measure the reward environment that produced the update.
They should not be upgraded into general reasoning or tool-use claims without
matched held-out evaluation.  The clean GSM8K control improves only
$82.0\% \to 83.3\%$ ($p = 0.26$), so we do not claim a significant held-out
math effect.  Held-out evaluation of the top-10 checkpoints (selected by
training last-10 reward) yields an 87.2--95.0\% accuracy band, but this is a
checkpoint-selection analysis: selecting by training reward introduces
over-optimism.  Four Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct seeds range 95.0--98.1\% training
last-10 but all land on 95.0\% held-out accuracy, showing that last-10
variance reflects Monte-Carlo sampling noise rather than genuine
generalization differences.

Tool-use, HumanEval, and MATH rows remain lower-tier evidence: tool-use
scores schema compliance (not executed success), the HumanEval sandbox
removes Python built-ins, and some prompt pools come from test splits used as
reward-environment probes.  These are useful as dynamics evidence and as sparse-
reward failure cases, not as proof of agentic capability.

\subsection{Claim 4: Algorithm labels are under-specified treatments.}
Algorithm-level claims require full-stack specification.  Under nominally
matched visible configurations (Qwen3-8B, $G=8$, lr$=10^{-5}$, GSM8K, 30
steps, seed 42), the Tinker runner reaches 84.4\% last-10 reward while TRL
on Modal H100 reaches 5.0\%.  This 17$\times$ gap does not prove Tinker is
better than TRL; it proves that ``same GRPO config'' is under-specified unless
backend, sampler, reward parser, LoRA configuration, optimizer,
checkpoint-selection rule, and evaluator are reported and controlled.  Our
Qwen PPO/GRPO comparison is artifact-sensitive (PPO last-10 differs between
ledger and statistical summary), and the Llama comparison favors PPO but is
single-seed and backend-confounded.  No universal PPO-versus-GRPO ranking
is supported.

\subsection{Contributions.}
This paper contributes:
(i)~a mixed-group probability mechanism explaining when a group-relative
RL loop has learning signal;
(ii)~ZVF/GU as early triage diagnostics separating cold-start collapse,
saturation, and usable contrast;
(iii)~a held-out GSM8K result showing that training reward conflates with
capability ($82.0\% \to 83.3\%$, $p = 0.26$);
(iv)~a methodological warning that PPO, GRPO, and DPO comparisons require
full-stack specification;
(v)~a diagnostic protocol for deciding whether a specific model--reward--
sampler--stack combination has usable group-relative RL signal.
We release the code, run logs, and checkpoints at
\url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/tinker-rl-lab}.
