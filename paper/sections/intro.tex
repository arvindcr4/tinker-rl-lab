\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reinforcement learning post-training now anchors many claims about language
model reasoning and alignment~\citep{ouyang2022training,guo2025deepseekr1nature}.
PPO~\citep{schulman2017proximal}, GRPO~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath}, and
DPO~\citep{rafailov2024direct} are often compared as if the algorithm label
were the full treatment.  In practice, the outcome also depends on the base
model, instruction tuning, reward parser, group construction, sampler,
backend, LoRA configuration, checkpoint selection, and evaluation harness.
This is the LLM post-training version of the reproducibility problem that
Henderson et al.~\citep{henderson2018deep} identified in classical deep RL.

\ours{} studies this problem as a systems question rather than as an
algorithm leaderboard.  The corpus contains 79 runs across structured
tool-call emission, GSM8K, MATH-style tasks, HumanEval-style binary
verification, small and frontier-scale model families, and several training
backends.  The results are intentionally uneven: some runs improve online
reward, some saturate, some collapse from step zero, and held-out GSM8K shows
only a small non-significant lift.  That heterogeneity is the point.  It lets
us ask which failures are genuine negative evidence and which failures simply
show that a sparse reward produced no usable contrast.

\paragraph{Claim 1: ZVF/GU are early triage diagnostics.}
The most reusable methodological contribution is the operational use of
Zero-Variance Fraction (ZVF) and Gradient Utilization (GU).  ZVF measures the
fraction of sampled GRPO groups whose rewards are all identical, and GU is its
complement.  High ZVF with low reward means cold-start collapse: all sampled
answers are wrong and the standard GRPO advantage is flat.  High ZVF with high
reward means saturation: the task may already be solved under the training
reward, and further rollouts waste compute.  Low ZVF means the current policy
still samples both successes and failures, so the group-relative update has
contrast to exploit.  In a 22-run de-duplicated validation set, a fixed rule
using only the first five logged steps catches both collapsed runs with no
false positives.  This is not enough to call ZVF a calibrated predictor.
Indeed, continuous ranking by early ZVF is weak.  The safe claim is narrower:
ZVF/GU are cheap early-run diagnostics that convert flat reward groups into
decisions to stop, warm-start, densify reward, change task difficulty, or
increase group size.

\paragraph{Claim 2: GRPO success depends on mixed-reward groups.}
Under binary rewards, a sampled group has zero variance if every completion is
wrong or every completion is correct.  If the current policy succeeds with
success rate $p_x$ for prompt $x$ and group size is $G$, the probability of a usable mixed
group is
\[
P(\text{usable}) \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_x \left[1 - (1-p_x)^G - p_x^G\right].
\]
The familiar $p_x > 1/G$ rule is only an expected-one-success heuristic, not a
threshold theorem.  This equation makes the central empirical pattern
legible.  SFT warm-up helps because it changes $p_0$; larger groups help only
when they reduce the all-wrong term more than they waste compute; dense or
partial-credit rewards help because they reduce all-zero/all-one groups.  The
tool-call positive result after SFT, strict no-warmup tool-call collapse,
HumanEval sparse-reward failure, and held-out GSM8K non-significance all fit
this same mechanism.  GRPO is therefore best understood as a
reward-contrast amplifier rather than a capability creator.

\paragraph{Claim 3: algorithm labels are incomplete treatments.}
The PPO/GRPO/DPO label is not enough to interpret an LLM post-training run.
Our Qwen PPO/GRPO comparison is artifact-sensitive: the source-of-truth
ledger records PPO at 0.225 last-10 reward, while a statistical summary
records 0.350.  The Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct comparison favors PPO over GRPO.
These observations should not be turned into a directional algorithm ranking.
They instead show that algorithm-level claims require stack-conditioned
reporting: model family, backend, reward implementation, rollout sampler,
optimizer and KL handling, LoRA target modules, checkpoint-selection rule, and
evaluation slice must be fixed or explicitly modeled.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper contributes (i) an operational ZVF/GU triage framing for standard
GRPO runs; (ii) a mixed-group probability mechanism that explains why
warm-starts, group size, reward density, and task difficulty govern whether
GRPO has signal; (iii) a source-of-truth ledger that separates online training
reward from held-out accuracy and flags artifact inconsistencies; and (iv) a
methodological warning that PPO, GRPO, and DPO comparisons in LLM
post-training are not identifiable unless the full training stack is reported.
We release the code, run logs, and checkpoints at
\url{https://github.com/arvindcr4/tinker-rl-lab}.
