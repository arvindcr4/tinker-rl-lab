\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reinforcement learning post-training now anchors many claims about language
model reasoning and alignment~\citep{ouyang2022training,guo2025deepseekr1nature}.
PPO~\citep{schulman2017proximal}, GRPO~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath}, and
DPO~\citep{rafailov2024direct} are often compared as if the algorithm label
were the full treatment.  In practice, the outcome also depends on the base
model, instruction tuning, reward parser, group construction, sampler,
backend, LoRA configuration, checkpoint selection, and evaluation harness.
This is the LLM post-training version of the reproducibility problem that
Henderson et al.~\citep{henderson2018deep} identified in classical deep RL.

\ours{} studies this problem as a systems audit rather than as an algorithm
leaderboard.  The corpus contains 79 runs across structured tool-call
emission, GSM8K, MATH-style tasks, HumanEval-style binary verification, small
and frontier-scale model families, and several training backends.  Before
presenting any results we note three deliberate scoping choices.  \emph{(i)
Algorithm fidelity.}  The training runner is a group-relative policy-gradient
variant: it retains group-normalized advantages and the critic-free structure
of GRPO, but it does not implement the PPO probability ratio and clip of
\citet{schulman2017proximal}, does not retain a frozen reference policy for
KL regularization, does one optimizer step per rollout batch, and does not
apply a completion-only token mask (a P1-A diagnostic on Qwen3.5-4B
measures 61.6\%--89.6\% of the full-sequence loss magnitude to come from
prompt tokens rather than from the completion).  Claims in
this paper therefore refer to this runner, not to canonical GRPO.
\emph{(ii) Evaluator scope.}  The HumanEval, tool-use, and MATH rewards
measure sandboxed execution with Python built-ins removed, JSON schema
compliance, and boxed-answer formatting respectively; they are proxies, not
end-to-end capability metrics.  \emph{(iii) Probe-vs.-generalization.}  The
MATH-500 and HumanEval prompt pools in the training loop are loaded from the
\texttt{test} splits and are used as reward-environment probes, not as
held-out generalization tests.  The only genuinely held-out capability
evaluation in this paper is a separate GSM8K 200-example slice that was not
used during training.  The remaining heterogeneity in outcomes across runs is
the point: it lets us ask which failures are evidence about the policy and
which failures are evidence about the reward environment.

\paragraph{Thesis.}
This paper does not show that GRPO broadly improves reasoning.  The clean
held-out GSM8K control shows only a small, non-significant gain over the base
model.  The thesis is narrower: in a critic-free group-relative RL loop, the
existence of a learning signal is governed by reward diversity inside sampled
groups.  ZVF/GU expose cold-start collapse and saturation early; training
reward is easy to overinterpret; and algorithm labels such as PPO, GRPO, and
DPO are not meaningful experimental treatments unless the full stack is
specified.  Held-out evaluations support capability claims, training rewards
support dynamics claims, and partial or proxy runs support hypotheses only.

\paragraph{Claim 1: reward diversity governs the learning signal.}
The most reusable methodological contribution is the operational use of
Zero-Variance Fraction (ZVF) and Gradient Utilization (GU).  ZVF measures the
fraction of sampled GRPO groups whose rewards are all identical, and GU is its
complement.  High ZVF with low reward means cold-start collapse: all sampled
answers are wrong and the standard GRPO advantage is flat.  High ZVF with high
reward means saturation: the task may already be solved under the training
reward, and further rollouts waste compute.  Low ZVF means the current policy
still samples both successes and failures, so the group-relative update has
contrast to exploit.  Under binary rewards, if the current policy succeeds
with success rate $p_x$ for prompt $x$ and group size is $G$, the probability
of a usable mixed group is
\[
P(\text{usable}) \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_x \left[1 - (1-p_x)^G - p_x^G\right].
\]
The familiar $p_x > 1/G$ rule is only an expected-one-success heuristic, not a
threshold theorem.  In a 22-run de-duplicated validation set, a fixed rule
using only the first five logged steps catches both collapsed runs with no
false positives.  Continuous ranking by early ZVF is weak, so the claim is
triage rather than forecasting: ZVF/GU convert flat reward groups into
decisions to stop, warm-start, densify reward, change task difficulty, or
increase group size.  GRPO is therefore best understood as a reward-contrast
amplifier rather than a capability creator.

\paragraph{Claim 2: algorithm labels are incomplete treatments.}
The PPO/GRPO/DPO label is not enough to interpret an LLM post-training run.
Our Qwen PPO/GRPO comparison is artifact-sensitive: the source-of-truth
ledger records PPO at 0.225 last-10 reward, while a statistical summary
records 0.350.  The Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct comparison favors PPO over GRPO.
These observations should not be turned into a directional algorithm ranking.
They instead show that algorithm-level claims require stack-conditioned
reporting: model family, backend, reward implementation, rollout sampler,
optimizer and KL handling, LoRA target modules, checkpoint-selection rule, and
evaluation slice must be fixed or explicitly modeled.

\paragraph{Claim 3: training reward is not capability.}
Online reward curves measure the reward environment that produced the update.
They should not be upgraded into general reasoning or tool-use claims without
matched held-out evaluation.  The clean GSM8K control improves only from
$82.0\%$ to $83.3\%$ ($p=0.26$), so the paper does not claim a significant
held-out math effect.  The positive tool-use rows measure JSON validity,
tool-name selection, and argument-key presence rather than executed tool
success; the HumanEval and MATH rows are similarly bounded by their sandbox
and answer-parser choices.  These results remain useful as dynamics evidence:
they show when the rollout distribution supplies reward contrast and when the
verifier or parser becomes the bottleneck.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper contributes (i) an operational ZVF/GU triage framing for standard
group-relative RL runs; (ii) a mixed-group probability mechanism that explains
why warm-starts, group size, reward density, and task difficulty govern whether
there is signal; (iii) a methodological warning that PPO, GRPO, and DPO
comparisons in LLM post-training are not identifiable unless the full training
stack is reported; and (iv) a source-of-truth ledger that separates online
training reward from held-out accuracy and flags artifact inconsistencies.
We release the code, run logs, and checkpoints at
\url{https://github.com/arvindcr4/tinker-rl-lab}.
