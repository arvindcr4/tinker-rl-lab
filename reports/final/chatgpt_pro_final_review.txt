Based on the state you gave, the paper still reads like a broad mechanistic claim paper, while the evidence really supports a narrow exploratory case study. The rejection story an adversarial AC can write is:

the only clean positive result is a single-pipeline custom-formatting gain; broader transfer is null; the “confirmed threshold” claim is confounded and partly contradicted by the 1.5B result; the MoE significance claim is not defensible from one MoE run; and several secondary findings are single-seed or N=1.

I’m quoting the claims exactly as you stated them, since that is the evidence I have access to here.

Title / Abstract / Main framing
1) Broad success framing is no longer supported

Severity: Critical

Evidence: “Tool calling: 0% to 92% JSON validity … custom eval, N=1 pipeline,” but “Held-out GSM8K … delta +1.3pp, p=0.26 (NOT significant)” and “Code … p=0.41 (NOT significant).”

Paste-ready fix:
“Abstract replacement sentence: In a single custom tool-calling pipeline, SFT+GRPO increases strict JSON validity from 0% to 92%; however, we do not observe statistically significant gains on out-of-domain GSM8K or on an exploratory 50-problem HumanEval subset.”

2) “Confirmed capacity threshold” is overclaimed

Severity: Critical

Evidence: “Capacity threshold (now labeled confirmed)” together with “Architecture confound: Llama-3.2-3B vs Qwen3.5-4B.”

Paste-ready fix:
“Replace every use of ‘confirmed capacity threshold’ with: suggestive model-family/scale transition in our low-budget grouped-RL setting.”

Or, as a full sentence:
“We observe a sharp performance discontinuity between the specific 3B and 4B models studied here, but because the comparison crosses model families, we interpret this as a suggestive model-family/scale transition in our setup rather than a confirmed universal capacity threshold.”

3) The 1.5B result and the 3B/4B threshold story currently conflict

Severity: Critical

Evidence: “Tool calling: 0% to 92% JSON validity … Qwen2.5-1.5B” versus “3B fails … 4B succeeds.”

Why an AC will hit this: as written, a reviewer can say your own 1.5B result already disproves your “4B threshold.”

Paste-ready fix:
“The 1.5B Qwen2.5 result and the 3B/4B comparison address different regimes: the former uses an SFT-initialized single-pipeline setup, whereas the latter probes grouped-RL behavior under a different optimization setting. We therefore do not combine these observations into a single monotonic parameter-count law.”

4) You are likely conflating JSON validity with correct tool use

Severity: Critical

Evidence: the headline metric is “92% JSON validity.”

Why an AC will hit this: valid JSON is necessary for tool execution, but not sufficient for correct tool choice or argument semantics.

Paste-ready fix:
“Throughout, we report strict JSON validity as a structural metric. This metric is necessary for executable tool calls, but it is not sufficient for correct tool selection or argument semantics.”

Table fix:
Rename any column labeled “Tool-calling accuracy” to:
“Valid JSON rate (strict parser pass)”

Footnote fix:
“A syntactically valid JSON object may still represent an incorrect tool call.”

5) The paper likely overstates generality from a single custom pipeline

Severity: High

Evidence: “custom eval, N=1 pipeline.”

Paste-ready fix:
“Our evaluation is tied to a single custom pipeline and schema. The paper should therefore be read as an internally controlled case study of learned structured-output compliance, not as a benchmark-competitive evaluation of tool use in general.”

6) A missing constrained-decoding acknowledgement will look like a major omission

Severity: Critical

Evidence: the main gain is parser-valid JSON, yet no mention of constrained decoding appears in your summary.

Why an AC will hit this: many reviewers will immediately ask why grammar-constrained decoding or native structured-output APIs were not the baseline.

Paste-ready fix:
“We study unconstrained generation. Grammar-constrained decoding and native structured-output APIs are practical alternatives for enforcing JSON well-formedness at inference time, so our results should be interpreted as learned format acquisition under unconstrained decoding rather than as the best deployable method for guaranteeing valid tool calls.”

Introduction / Contributions
7) The contributions are probably too broad for 25 total runs

Severity: High

Evidence: one paper is trying to claim tool-calling improvement, a capacity threshold, MoE volatility, group-size scaling, cross-seed stability, SFT+GRPO complementarity, synthetic-real gap, and transfer behavior from “25 Tinker runs + Colab QLoRA.”

Paste-ready fix:
“Rather than a comprehensive scaling study, we present a low-budget exploratory case study. Our central empirical contribution is a narrow positive result on one tool-calling pipeline; the model-size, group-size, MoE, and synthetic-versus-real observations are secondary descriptive findings.”

8) “SFT+GRPO complementarity” is too strong for N=1 evidence

Severity: High

Evidence: “SFT+GRPO complementarity (N=1 each for 1.5B and 3B).”

Paste-ready fix:
“In two pilot configurations, the combined SFT→GRPO recipe outperformed either stage alone. We present this as suggestive evidence for complementarity, not as a general claim that both stages are necessary across models or tasks.”

9) The synthetic-real gap is probably overgeneralized

Severity: High

Evidence: “Synthetic-real gap: 3-8x difficulty difference.”

Why an AC will hit this: “difficulty” sounds universal; your evidence sounds setup-specific.

Paste-ready fix:
“In our pipeline, the real task appears to require roughly 3–8× more optimization budget than the synthetic proxy. We interpret this as a setup-specific proxy-mismatch ratio rather than as a universal statement about synthetic versus real data.”

10) Cost framing can make the work look underpowered rather than efficient

Severity: Medium

Evidence: “$55–65 total budget” is prominent in your summary.

Paste-ready fix:
“All experiments were conducted under a deliberately small budget ($55–65 total). This improves accessibility, but it also limits the breadth and certainty of the conclusions.”

Methods / Experimental Setup
11) Cross-model comparisons are badly confounded beyond architecture alone

Severity: High

Evidence: “Architecture confound (Llama vs Qwen),” plus “LoRA rank 8-64,” “50-100 steps,” “QLoRA,” and multiple model sizes.

Why an AC will hit this: even if you already mention the family confound, reviewers can still say the comparison is also not budget-matched or hyperparameter-matched.

Paste-ready fix:
“Between-model comparisons are not architecture-controlled or budget-matched: models differ in family, parameter count, adapter rank, and in some cases optimization budget. We therefore treat cross-model differences as descriptive rather than causal.”

12) The low-budget regime creates a strong alternative explanation for the threshold result

Severity: High

Evidence: “batch=2, 50-100 steps, no KL regularization,” and “no entropy logging.”

Why an AC will hit this: the 3B failure could be optimization instability or collapse, not “insufficient capacity.”

Paste-ready fix:
“All conclusions are specific to a short-budget QLoRA regime (batch size 2, 50–100 steps, no KL regularization). In this regime, poor 3B performance may reflect optimization instability or collapse as much as insufficient capacity.”

13) You need a stronger limitation on mechanism claims

Severity: High

Evidence: “no reward function ablation,” “no PPO/REINFORCE baselines,” “no KL/entropy logging for main experiments.”

Paste-ready fix:
“Because we do not ablate reward components, compare against alternative policy-gradient baselines, or log KL/entropy in the main runs, we cannot make strong mechanism claims about why the observed improvements occur. In particular, we cannot cleanly distinguish productive credit assignment from reward over-optimization or mode collapse.”

Results: tool-calling, threshold, MoE, scaling

Severity: Medium

“Rather than saying the 4B model ‘succeeds,’ state: the 4B model can reach high performance in this setting, but seed-to-seed variability remains nontrivial (84.7% mean, 12.0 s.d., 4 seeds).”

20) The MoE variance claim is statistically indefensible as stated

Severity: Critical

Evidence: “MoE volatility: 2.43x variance, Levene p=7e-6 (single MoE run vs 5 dense seeds).”

Why an AC will hit this: one MoE run is not enough to make a variance-comparison claim across runs. If the p-value comes from within-run samples, the independence assumptions are likely broken.

Paste-ready fix:
“Because we observed only one MoE run, we treat the MoE comparison as anecdotal and do not interpret it as a statistically established architecture effect. The MoE trace appears noisier than our dense runs, but this observation requires replication.”

Table fix:
Replace any row like
“MoE variance: 2.43×, Levene p=7e-6”
with
“MoE volatility (single-run anecdote): visually/empirically noisier than dense runs; no inferential test reported.”

21) The group-size scaling claim is single-seed and should not read like a law

Severity: High

Paste-ready fix:
“For one 8B seed (42), performance rises from 23.8% at G=4 to 54.7% at G=32. We present this as an illustrative seed-fixed scaling curve, not as evidence of seed-robust monotonic improvement.”

22) The group-size story and the threshold story need an interaction caveat

Severity: High

Paste-ready fix:
“The apparent benefit of larger group size at 8B does not transfer downward automatically: in our runs, the 3B model remains near floor at both small and large G. We therefore interpret group size as interacting strongly with model family/scale rather than as a uniform global improvement.”

23) The cross-seed stability claim is descriptive only
