NeurIPS Area Chair Review — GRPO for Agentic LLM Fine-Tuning
Reviewer: Claude (Sonnet 4.6), acting as Area Chair
Date: 2026-04-04
==============================================================

Q1: Why were only 50 HumanEval items used instead of full 164, and does this
subset selection introduce cherry-picking bias inflating 32%->40%?

The paper describes this as a "preliminary 50-problem subset" but provides no
justification for which 50 items were selected or whether selection was random,
stratified, or convenience-based. At n=50, each problem represents 2 percentage
points, so the 8-point gain (16/50 -> 20/50) is literally four problems — an
effect size that sits entirely within the bootstrap 95% CI [27%, 53%] the
authors themselves report. Without confirmation that the 50 items were drawn
uniformly at random from the full 164 before any training was run, cherry-picking
bias cannot be ruled out. The minimum fix is to state the exact random seed used
to subsample, or run the full 164-item evaluation; until then this result should
not be foregrounded as evidence of GRPO effectiveness on code.

---

Q2: Custom JSON tool-call metric — have they validated scoring fidelity against
human judgment? What validation is minimum?

No validation against human judgment is reported anywhere in the paper or
supplementary. The supplementary explicitly acknowledges: "We did not measure
inter-rater reliability, did not use multiple human judges." The three-component
rubric (JSON validity 0.3, tool name 0.4, arguments present 0.3) is a proxy
for task success, not a measure of it — a model could score 0.7 by producing
valid JSON with the right tool name but completely wrong arguments. The minimum
acceptable validation is: (a) human agreement rate on a random sample of
~100 scored completions (Cohen's kappa >= 0.8 target), and (b) correlation of
rubric score with end-task success (e.g., does the tool call actually execute
correctly). Without this, the 0%->92% headline figure is uninterpretable.

---

Q3: With only 50 training steps, how to rule out 0%->92% tool-call improvement
reflects overfitting?

The paper cannot rule it out in its current form. The tool-call evaluation is
performed on "training-prompt rollouts" from the same prompt distribution used
for GRPO updates — there is no held-out tool-call test set. With only 200 RL
prompts from Glaive-v2 and G=4-16 group size, 50 steps see the same prompts
multiple times, so the model has ample opportunity to memorize specific prompt-
completion patterns rather than learning generalizable tool-use. The diagnostic
would be straightforward: evaluate on 50 held-out Glaive-v2 prompts (or ToolBench
scenarios) not seen during training. The paper's own supplementary warns "our
primary evaluation metrics are computed on training prompts with new rollouts,
not on held-out test sets" — this is precisely the overfitting risk.

---

Q4: SFT+GRPO complementarity — was ordering (SFT-then-GRPO vs GRPO-then-SFT)
tested?

No. Every experiment that combines SFT and GRPO uses SFT-then-GRPO ordering only.
The paper claims SFT+GRPO complementarity as a novel finding, but complementarity
in one ordering direction is a weaker claim than true synergy: it is well-
established that SFT warm-starts improve RL training by narrowing the initial
exploration space. GRPO-then-SFT (or interleaved SFT-GRPO-SFT) is not tested,
so the "complementarity" finding is really "SFT is a useful initializer for GRPO"
— an already-known result from ReST, STaR, and related work. The paper should
either test the reverse ordering or reframe this as a confirmation rather than
a novel finding.

---

Q5: How were hyperparameters selected across 17 runs — grid/random/manual?

The paper does not state this explicitly. The supplementary shows that LR values
{3e-5, 5e-6, 1e-4}, group sizes {4, 8, 16}, and temperatures {0.4, 0.8, 1.0, 1.2}
appear across runs, but no systematic grid or random search protocol is described.
Given the run IDs and configurations, this appears to be manual exploration driven
by partial results — effectively OFAT (one-factor-at-a-time) tuning, which is known
to miss interaction effects. More critically, there is no mention of a held-out
validation set used to stop hyperparameter search; peak training reward was the
apparent criterion, which introduces overfitting to training dynamics. A Methods
subsection explicitly stating the hyperparameter selection protocol is required for
reproducibility.

---

Q6: Training reward 30.5% as single number — was it measured across seeds?

Yes and no. The 30.5% is the mean of the Last-10-Avg training rewards across 5
seeds (27.5%, 27.5%, 32.5%, 30.0%, 35.0%), with SD=3.3% (reported in the paper).
This is methodologically appropriate. However, the abstract also reports "SD=2.2%"
which refers to the held-out test accuracy distribution (83.3%), not the training
reward — the paper conflates these two standard deviations in the abstract and
conclusion, which will confuse readers. The training reward of 30.5% (SD=3.3%)
is well-characterized; the problem is that it dramatically underestimates the
actual policy quality because it is measured under high-temperature stochastic
sampling during training, not greedy decoding. The paper explains this discrepancy
but does not fix the abstract.

---

Q7: 3B-4B capacity threshold from few model sizes — should they fit threshold
regression with CIs?

Yes, strongly. The "capacity threshold" claim rests on exactly two data points:
3B (2.34% accuracy) and 4B (82.5% accuracy), plus a third at 8B (83.3%). There
is no 3.5B, no repeat of 4B with a different architecture, and no sweep between
3B and 4B. A step-function capacity threshold is an extraordinary claim that
requires either (a) testing 3.5B, 3.8B, etc. to locate the break, or (b) fitting
a logistic or sigmoid regression on {model_size, accuracy} with confidence
intervals to show the inflection. As written, the 3B-4B threshold could equally
be explained by the confound that the 3B and 4B models are different architectures
(Llama-3.2-3B vs. Qwen3.5-4B) and different platforms (Colab vs. Tinker), making
the comparison unreliable. The paper partially acknowledges this in the analysis
section but still foregrounds it as a novel finding.

---

Q8: MoE routing volatility 2.43x — what metric and is it statistically
significant?

The metric is step-to-step reward variance, reported as SD 0.29 (MoE) vs. 0.12
(Dense), ratio 2.43x. The supplementary reports a Levene's test result:
F(1,98)=22.4, p=7.0e-6, confirming the variance difference is statistically
significant at n=50 steps each. This is the most statistically rigorous result
in the paper. However, the Qwen3-30B-MoE results (99% peak, 95% final accuracy)
are not reported in the main paper's run registry table, and it is unclear whether
the MoE run used identical hyperparameters to the dense 8B comparison — if LR or
group size differed, the variance comparison is confounded. The paper should add
the MoE run to the main run registry with full hyperparameters, and explicitly
state that all confounds were controlled.

---

Q9: Code improvement 32%->40% on 50 items = 4 problems. Significant at p<0.05?

No. Under a standard binomial proportion test (H0: p=0.32, observed 20/50=0.40),
the one-sided p-value is approximately 0.16, well above the p<0.05 threshold. The
authors' own bootstrap 95% CI [27%, 53%] post-GRPO explicitly brackets the
pre-GRPO 32% baseline, confirming the improvement is not statistically
distinguishable from noise. The paper correctly states "we do not claim statistical
significance" in the text, but the abstract and conclusion table list this result
alongside the well-powered GSM8K results without adequate warning. Given that
exactly 4 additional problems were solved, this result should either be removed
from primary claims or explicitly labeled as "inconclusive" in every location
where it appears, including the abstract.

---

Q10: Cross-seed stability SD=2.2% — compared to published GRPO baselines?

The paper does not compare its SD=2.2% (held-out test) or SD=3.3% (training
reward) against published GRPO variance figures. DeepSeekMath reports a single
run at 88.2% GSM8K, providing no seed variance for comparison. DAPO and Dr.GRPO
papers similarly report limited seed replication. The 5-seed replication is
genuinely stronger than most published GRPO work, but without a baseline
comparison, calling SD=2.2% "cross-seed stability" as a novel finding is
overclaiming — it is a measurement rather than a finding. The authors should
compare to the variance one would expect from simple sampling noise at n=200
(binomial SE at 83.3% is approximately 2.6%), which suggests the across-seed
SD=2.2% is within the range expected by chance, not evidence of unusual stability.

---

Q11: ToolBench, APIBench, ToolAlpaca not cited — how does 0%->92% compare?

These benchmarks are not cited for comparison, and the 0%->92% figure cannot be
placed in context without them. ToolBench (Wang et al. 2023) reports win rates
and API call success rates for instruction-tuned models on standardized multi-step
scenarios; APIBench evaluates hallucination rates on ~16k API calls; ToolAlpaca
assesses generalization to unseen tools. The paper's 92% JSON validity on
Glaive-v2 training rollouts is a format compliance metric, not an end-task success
metric — it is not comparable to any of these benchmarks' metrics. The Related
Work section cites ToolRM and FC-RewardBench as "standardized tool-calling
evaluation absent from our custom rubric," implicitly acknowledging the gap, but
does not quantify how large the gap is or attempt even a qualitative mapping.

---

Q12: How to position GSM8K findings against DeepSeekMath and DAPO variants?

The paper's 83.3% held-out GSM8K is below DeepSeekMath's 88.2% (7B, G=64, full
training) and below Common7B's 82.6% via SFT alone. The paper acknowledges the
SFT-only baseline but does not run it as an ablation — meaning it cannot
demonstrate GRPO adds anything over SFT for math reasoning with this setup. DAPO
and Dr.GRPO configurations are implemented in a supplementary Colab notebook but
not benchmarked on GSM8K held-out test with comparable seeds, so no empirical
comparison is possible. The positioning should be honest: 83.3% with LoRA on
Qwen3-8B after 50 steps is a reasonable practitioner result but not a state-of-
the-art claim. The paper should either run the SFT-only ablation or explicitly
note that GRPO's marginal contribution over SFT on math is unknown.

---

Q13: SFT+GRPO echoes ReST, STaR — how to differentiate?

The paper does not cite ReST (Gulcehre et al. 2023) or STaR (Zeiler et al. 2022)
in the Related Work, which is a significant omission. Both methods use iterative
self-improvement where model outputs become the next round's training data,
producing a similar "SFT warms up RL" dynamic. The paper's SFT+GRPO
complementarity finding would need to demonstrate: (a) that the benefit persists
when SFT and GRPO are run on the same data distribution, not sequential datasets;
(b) that GRPO adds a qualitatively different signal (online exploration) rather
than simply more gradient steps. Without ReST/STaR as baselines and without
testing GRPO-only vs. SFT-only vs. SFT+GRPO with matched compute, the
differentiation is asserted rather than demonstrated.

---

Q14: Concurrent work STILL/Tiny-GRPO target sub-3B — impact on novelty?

The paper does not cite STILL or Tiny-GRPO. If these concurrent works target
sub-3B models and report capacity thresholds, the 3B-4B finding would be a
confirmation rather than a novel discovery. More broadly, the concurrent work
landscape (Dr.GRPO, DAPO, S-GRPO, StepGRPO, RLOO, REINFORCE++) is cited in
the paper, but without a systematic comparison table showing what each work
demonstrates about small-model GRPO, the novelty boundary is undefined. The
paper should add a "concurrent work" paragraph explicitly listing which findings
have been reported elsewhere at sub-8B scale, and distinguish its contributions
accordingly. The 5-seed replication on a single benchmark with held-out evaluation
is likely the most defensible novel contribution.

---

Q15: MoE volatility contextualized against Switch Transformer/GLaM — is 2.43x
surprising?

The paper does not cite Switch Transformer (Fedus et al. 2022), GLaM (Du et al.
2022), or any MoE training stability literature. The 2.43x variance increase
during RL fine-tuning of a MoE model is plausible given known MoE training
instability (router collapse, load imbalance, gradient spikes at expert
boundaries), but "surprising" cannot be assessed without a baseline expectation.
Switch Transformer papers document training instability requiring careful LR
scheduling and gradient clipping; GLaM uses auxiliary load-balancing losses
precisely because router variance compounds training noise. Against this
background, 2.43x is perhaps expected or even conservative. The paper proposes
"non-smooth loss surfaces" as the mechanism but does not measure routing entropy,
expert load imbalance, or gradient norm spikes that would confirm this.

---

Q16: 'Agentic LLM fine-tuning' never defined — what operational definition?

The term appears in the title and abstract but is never operationally defined.
The paper covers three tasks: structured JSON tool calling, code generation via
pass@1, and math reasoning on GSM8K. Of these, only tool calling has a
meaningfully "agentic" character (sequential tool selection within an episode);
code generation and math reasoning are single-turn tasks with no environmental
feedback loop or multi-step decision making. A working definition might be:
"fine-tuning LLMs to execute multi-step tasks requiring dynamic tool selection
and sequential decision-making under verifiable environmental feedback." Under
this definition, only the multi-turn tool chaining experiment qualifies, and the
title overclaims. The paper should either (a) adopt and justify a definition that
encompasses all three tasks, or (b) retitle to "GRPO for Small LLM Post-Training."

---

Q17: Novel vs. confirmatory criteria implicit — should add Methods subsection?

Yes. The paper distinguishes "four novel findings" from "three empirical
confirmations" but the criteria for this classification are never stated. The
reader cannot evaluate whether capacity thresholds at 3B-4B are novel (they
resemble known "emergent abilities" literature), whether MoE volatility is novel
(it echoes Switch Transformer findings), or whether two-phase learning is
confirmatory (it resembles curriculum learning observations). A 1-paragraph
Methods subsection titled "Novelty Classification Criteria" should state: what
evidence threshold distinguishes novel from confirmatory, whether a literature
search was conducted to verify novelty, and how concurrent work was assessed.
Without this, the four-vs-three split reads as author preference rather than
principled analysis.

---

Q18: Are figure captions self-contained per NeurIPS standards?

Partially. The main paper has three figures. Fig. 1 caption mentions "(a)
Training-set reward trajectories... (b) Zero-loss origins" but the panel labels
and what "not shown" means for the 3B model are unclear without the figure text.
Fig. 2 caption references "Section 5.3" for context, which is a cross-reference
dependency that violates self-containedness. Fig. 3 caption is adequate but uses
undefined notation ("$>$0.8 reward" is ambiguous without knowing the reward
scale). NeurIPS guidelines require that captions be understandable without
reading the main text. The supplementary table captions are generally better.
The fix is mechanical: add the axis labels, metric definitions, and model names
directly into each caption.

---

Q19: Paper conflates synthetic-real gap with train-test discrepancy — how to
separate?

The paper presents both: (a) the synthetic-vs-real data gap (5-tool synthetic
reaches >0.9 reward, xlam-60k stays <0.36), and (b) the train-test discrepancy
(30.5% training reward vs. 83.3% held-out accuracy on GSM8K). These are
fundamentally different phenomena — the first is a data distribution difficulty
gap, the second is an evaluation protocol artifact from high-temperature stochastic
sampling during training. The paper discusses them in adjacent subsections (3.3
and 4.2.2) without distinguishing them conceptually. The confusion matters because
the synthetic-real gap is a genuine generalization concern, while the
training-reward underestimate is an instrumentation artifact. A clear framing
would be: Section X addresses data domain transfer; Section Y addresses training
metric reliability. These should not share the heading "evaluation limitations."

---

Q20: Should add config table mapping each result to exact hyperparameters?

Yes, this is essential for reproducibility and is currently absent from the main
paper. The supplementary run registry (Tables A1-A3) provides run IDs and some
parameters, but the mapping from each quantitative claim in the main paper to its
exact hyperparameter configuration is not direct. For example, the 83.3% held-out
GSM8K uses "LoRA rank 32" and "50 steps" but the exact LR (3e-5), group size (8),
and temperature (1.0) are only findable by cross-referencing run IDs in the
supplementary. A single consolidated table — one row per main paper claim, columns
for model, LR, rank, group size, temperature, steps, seed, platform — would
eliminate ambiguity and is standard practice for NeurIPS reproducibility.

---

Q21: 3B-4B threshold from 2 data points — reframe as hypothesis?

Strongly agree. The evidence is: Llama-3.2-3B on Colab fails (2.34%), Qwen3.5-4B
on Tinker succeeds (82.5%). These are different model families, different
platforms, and different base capability levels — Llama-3.2-3B and Qwen3.5-4B
are not architecturally comparable checkpoints on a single scaling axis. The paper
should reframe this from "we identify a capacity threshold between 3B and 4B" to
"we hypothesize a capacity threshold in the 3B-4B range, pending controlled
ablations with matched architectures and platforms." This is not a cosmetic change:
as written, reviewers will correctly flag the confound and may reject on this basis
alone. The hypothesis framing is honest and does not require new experiments.

---

Q22: Should abstract foreground 3 novel claims specifically?

Yes. The current abstract lists all results sequentially without clearly
distinguishing the four novel findings from the three confirmations, and includes
methodological caveats mid-sentence ("custom evaluation," "50-problem subset")
that reduce clarity. For a NeurIPS abstract, the structure should be: (1) one
sentence on what problem is studied, (2) two sentences on the strongest novel
findings with their evidence, (3) one sentence on limitations. The three most
defensible novel claims are: (a) 5-seed held-out GSM8K replication at 83.3%
(SD=2.2%) — the only result with proper held-out evaluation, (b) MoE variance
is 2.43x dense with statistical significance (p=7e-6), and (c) the 3B-4B capacity
boundary (framed as a hypothesis). The abstract should lead with these rather than
burying them after the 0%->92% tool-call figure.

---

Q23: MoE volatility purely observational — propose mechanism?

The paper proposes "non-smooth loss surfaces from MoE routing" as a qualitative
mechanism but provides no supporting measurement. Three testable mechanisms are:
(a) router entropy collapse — if the router concentrates on few experts, small
parameter perturbations redirect many tokens, amplifying reward variance; (b)
expert load imbalance — if a few experts carry most tokens, their gradient updates
are large and variable; (c) GRPO's within-group normalization interacts with
sparse activation, since different group members may activate different expert
subsets, making the advantage normalization within-group less meaningful. The
supplementary notes "routing entropy or expert-load imbalance measurements" were
not collected, which is the core gap. At minimum, the paper should propose one
of these as a falsifiable hypothesis for future work, rather than stopping at
observation.

---

Q24: Cross-seed stability = standard practice, not finding — reframe?

Correct. Running multiple seeds and reporting standard deviation is a basic
reproducibility practice recommended in the ML community since Loshchilov &
Hutter (2017) and institutionalized by NeurIPS reproducibility checklists. The
paper elevates this to a "novel finding" which will draw skeptical reviewer
comments. The reframe should be: "We confirm GRPO reproducibility with 5-seed
replication (SD=2.2% held-out test accuracy), satisfying NeurIPS reproducibility
standards." This positions the multi-seed work as a methodological strength
(which it genuinely is relative to most single-run GRPO papers) without
overclaiming novelty. The actual insight — that zero-loss rates vary from 16-28%
across seeds, implying exploration is stochastic in a meaningful way — is more
interesting and could be elevated instead.

---

Q25: Unified mechanistic narrative vs. separate per-task reports?

The paper currently reads as three independent experiment logs (tool calling, code
generation, math reasoning) connected by the GRPO method name but not by a
shared mechanistic story. A unified narrative would observe that all three tasks
share the same GRPO training dynamic — format compliance precedes reasoning
improvement, group saturation creates zero-loss regimes, and model capacity
determines whether the zero-loss regime is productive (all-correct) or
pathological (all-wrong). This "capacity-modulated group saturation" story could
unify the findings: small models saturate into all-wrong regimes and stall; large
models saturate into all-correct regimes and plateau; mid-range models (4B-8B)
maintain productive mixed groups. Framing the paper around this mechanism, with
task-specific results as evidence, would transform it from a run report into a
mechanistic contribution and substantially strengthen the NeurIPS submission.
